# Research: NUM-02 and paving-task precedents

## Summary
The two goals are established but mathematically different task families. **A** asks pupils to infer an unknown *number of objects* by comparing it with a visible/known numerosity benchmark; **B** asks pupils to quantify a *continuous magnitude* by repeating one equal unit end-to-end (length) or covering a surface without gaps/overlaps (area), then relate that result to a conventional unit. A paving scenario is a standard **measurement/tiling family (B)**, not NUM-02/A—even if pupils finally count the pavers.

## Findings

### Curriculum boundary

1. **PER keeps cardinality estimation and measurement distinct.** MSN 22 includes “Estimation du nombre d’objets d’une collection” and organized counting/grouping of collections; MSN 24 separately specifies organizing a measurement, choosing a conventional or non-conventional unit and procedure for length/area, plus estimating magnitudes. This supports the proposed A/B boundary rather than treating both as generic estimation. [CIIP/PER MSN 22](https://per.ciip.ch/api/files/274) · [CIIP/PER MSN 24](https://per.ciip.ch/api/files/18)

### Strong precedents for A — unknown cardinality from a benchmark collection

2. **Illustrative Mathematics, “The circled group shows 10 beans.”** *Pupil problem/action:* inspect a bean collection in which one circled subset is explicitly identified as 10; give estimates that are too low, about right and too high, discuss with a partner, then reason in tens and ones. *Learning target:* “estimating a reasonable answer based on experience and known information” and using place value to estimate object quantities. *Supports:* **A directly**: a known benchmark collection is embedded in the unknown collection. *Must not be copied:* the bean/plate image, exact wording, three-column worksheet, or lesson flow; retain only the precedent-grounded mathematical structure (known 10 used multiplicatively/additively to estimate an unknown cardinality). [Illustrative Mathematics Grade 1, Unit 4, Lesson 23](https://im.kendallhunt.com/k5/teachers/grade-1/unit-4/lesson-23/lesson.html)

3. **NRICH, “Count the Crayons.”** *Pupil problem/action:* briefly inspect pictures of pencils, estimate each total, explain and compare methods; the official getting-started prompt says to imagine grouping the items into 10s and allows a range (example: about 50; 40–60). *Learning target:* beginning estimation by grouping in tens, accepting multiple reasonable answers, and justifying method. *Supports:* **A strongly**, with an internally constructed benchmark group of 10 rather than a separately supplied reference set. *Must not be copied:* pencil photographs, exact question sequence, example values, or reveal format; also do not turn it into exact counting before the estimate. [NRICH: Count the Crayons](https://nrich.maths.org/problems/count-crayons)

4. **NRICH, “Estimation Station.”** *Pupil problem/action:* estimate objects in a transparent jar, justify more/less or interval judgments, then count to check; compare with yesterday’s known collection and vary object/container size. *Learning target:* estimation, counting/cardinality above 10, comparison and ordering of estimates, and linking object size/density to quantity. *Supports:* **A as a recurring calibration routine**, especially when yesterday’s counted jar is retained as the known benchmark. *Must not be copied:* jar-station dressing, daily contest/“closest guess” framing, materials list, or exact prompts; importantly, a same-size benchmark is needed if the target is cardinality comparison rather than reasoning mainly about container volume. [NRICH: Estimation Station](https://nrich.maths.org/content/id/13339/Estimation%20Station2%20J.pdf)

5. **Research precedent for explicit numerical reference points.** Baroody and Gatzke asked kindergarten-age children to estimate dot-set sizes (3–35), judge sets against stated reference points 5, 10 or 20, and locate sets between two reference points. This confirms that benchmark-assisted set-size estimation is an established numerosity-estimation form, not a measurement task. *Supports:* **A**. *Must not be copied:* the study’s assessment battery, gifted-sample framing, stimuli, or thresholds; it is evidence for the task family, not a ready-made classroom mechanic. [Journal for Research in Mathematics Education, DOI](https://doi.org/10.5951/jresematheduc.22.1.0059)

### Strong precedents for B — iterating/tiling a unit, then conventional length

6. **Math Learning Center, “Measuring Length in Teacher Feet / Giant Feet.”** *Pupil problem/action:* estimate the classroom width, measure heel-to-toe, notice different pupils obtain different counts, adopt one equal paper “teacher foot,” and repeatedly move that single unit with each new start placed at the previous endpoint; a follow-up uses a yard-long “giant foot.” *Learning target:* need for equal/standard units, iterative use of one tool for an object longer than the tool, no gaps, and the inverse relation between unit size and count. *Supports:* **B directly**, including the transition from non-conventional to conventional units. *Must not be copied:* teacher-foot/giant story, worksheets, imperial units, classroom path, or narrative. A 50 cm paver can occupy the same mathematical role, but the design must preserve endpoint marking and equal-unit iteration. [Math Learning Center, Grade 2 Measurement: Length](https://www.mathlearningcenter.org/sites/default/files/pdfs/SecB2SUP-D2_MeasLengCust-201304.pdf)

7. **Math Learning Center, “Measuring the Area of Paper Rectangles.”** *Pupil problem/action:* estimate how many equal square units cover a rectangle, physically cover it “without leaving any holes, gaps, or overlaps,” determine totals efficiently by rows/groups, and compare estimates with the measured area. A follow-up estimates and measures classroom surfaces using larger paper squares. *Learning target:* area as the number of same-sized area units covering a shape; estimation and measurement with non-standard units. *Supports:* **B directly for 2D paving/tiling**. *Must not be copied:* rectangles, base-ten tiles, worksheets, dimensions, or row counts; and do not collapse area into merely counting a pre-drawn collection—the unit must cover the surface. [Math Learning Center, Grade 3 Measurement: Area](https://www.mathlearningcenter.org/sites/default/files/pdfs/SecB3SUP-D2_MeasureArea-201304.pdf)

8. **Illustrative Mathematics, equal units with no gaps/overlaps.** *Pupil problem/action:* evaluate peers’ shoe measurements made with cubes, deciding that color is irrelevant but equal cube length and no gaps/overlaps are essential; related practice lays length units end-to-end and compares measurements made with different-size units. *Learning target:* measure length by iterating same-size units accurately and distinguish a unit’s physical size from irrelevant appearance. *Supports:* **B**. *Must not be copied:* shoe/cube characters, images, exact dialogue or lesson sequence. [Illustrative Mathematics Grade 1, Unit 6, Lesson 7](https://curriculum.illustrativemathematics.org/k5/teachers/grade-1/unit-6/lesson-7/lesson.html)

### Decision on the paving scenario

9. **Yes: it is a known task family, and it fits B.** Repeated equal “pavers” placed end-to-end instantiate unit iteration for **length**; equal pavers laid to cover a region instantiate **area tiling**. A conventional 50 cm paver is a conventional-length referent/tool: pupils can first estimate how many 50 cm placements span the path, then measure by iterating it without gaps/overlaps, and express the resulting length conventionally. Counting placements is the numerical result of measurement; it does **not** make the task unknown-cardinality estimation (A). If pavers have width and cover a whole path, state whether the target is linear run (one dimension) or surface area (two dimensions), because those are not interchangeable.

## Design guardrails
- Keep **A** about *how many objects are already there*: comparison to a known 10/20/etc. collection, an approximate total/range, explanation, then optional exact count for feedback.
- Keep **B** about *how much length/area*: one invariant physical unit, repeated with endpoint continuity or tiled coverage, explicit checks for gaps/overlaps, then connection to 50 cm/metres (or another conventional unit).
- Do not use the same success criterion for both. A rewards reasonable benchmark-based approximation; B requires spatial conservation and correct unit iteration/coverage.
- Do not infer that a paving skin makes an A task: if the child is covering/measuring a path, the governing mathematics remains B.

## Gaps
- No primary source found using the exact object and dimension “50 cm paver.” The classification is nevertheless well supported by first-party tasks using a foot/yard unit and square tiles; the paver is a contextual substitution, not evidence for a new mechanic.
- The accessible PER PDFs establish the strand distinction, but the search did not expose a CIIP-published pupil task matching the exact benchmark-collection setup. IM and NRICH provide the strongest inspectable pupil-task precedents.

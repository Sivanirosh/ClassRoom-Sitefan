# EX-0303 — Le cabinet des regards

## Decision summary

Un même lieu proche n’est pas décrit de la même manière par tous les acteurs : ce qu’un acteur met au premier plan dépend notamment de son rôle dans la situation. Le prototype fait composer un profil d’acteur, puis orienter une « lentille » vers le point de vue soutenu par une courte fiche de rôle ; la représentation du lieu se réorganise immédiatement pour montrer la conséquence du choix avant sa soumission. Cette construction mérite un essai auprès d’élèves parce qu’elle coordonne catégorie, rôle et représentation au lieu de demander seulement de réciter des étiquettes d’acteurs.

### Design interpretation

The PER does not prescribe a lens interface, these fictional dossiers, a two-part response, the order of variations or any automated correction. The design hypothesis is that selecting a category and then watching a chosen concern move to the foreground will make the relationship **actor category → situated role evidence → point of view** inspectable. A category is deliberately not treated as a machine that predicts an opinion: the valid point of view always depends on the explicit acts and words in the current dossier.

### What this exercise does not establish

The prototype does not establish mastery, a stable belief about real social groups, the full end-of-Cycle-2 expectation, independent description of every actor category, map-reading skill, conflict resolution, or the pupil’s ability to state their own position without support. It does not claim that all members of a category share one view. Completion and three correct submissions are technical loop outcomes, not proof of learning or pupil validation.

## Learning objective

Given a short fictional dossier about an actor and a nearby place, the pupil composes the actor’s category and selects the point of view supported by an explicit role clue, revising either part when the resulting relationship is incoherent.

The observable unit is the **paired construction**: a category plus a role-supported point of view. One isolated category click or completion alone is not sufficient evidence.

## Prerequisites and vocabulary

### Prerequisites

- Distinguish one person from a group or organisation.
- Read a short first-person statement and locate who acts, what they do and where.
- Recognise that one place can support several uses.
- Select a button and submit a response; no drag convention is required.

### Necessary vocabulary, defined in the introduction and kept available

- **Acteur / actrice :** a person or group that acts on, decides about or uses a place.
- **Catégorie :** a way to locate an actor — individual or collective, public or private, and here social, economic or political.
- **Point de vue / regard :** what this actor puts in the foreground in this precise situation.
- **Public :** connected to a public authority and shared rules; **private :** a person or non-government organisation.
- **Social :** daily and shared life; **economic :** buying, selling or supplying; **political :** collective decisions and rules.

The interface states that a category does not determine an opinion on its own.

## Misconceptions and productive difficulty

No response pattern below is asserted as a documented misconception; the PER source supports the curricular content, not these causal claims. They are **design hypotheses / possible strategies to observe**:

1. The pupil may choose the concern they personally prefer rather than the concern supported by the actor’s role statement.
2. The pupil may classify any named group as public, or any named person as representing only themselves, without reading who decides or owns the organisation.
3. The pupil may use a keyword shortcut such as “commerce = voitures” while ignoring a contradictory dossier clue.
4. The pupil may infer that every actor in one category must have the same point of view.
5. The pupil may solve the category and point-of-view parts independently without checking whether they form one coherent relationship.

The productive difficulty is coordinating three kinds of evidence: who the actor is, what role the actor performs, and what the actor explicitly brings to the foreground. Accidental difficulty would include remembering hidden definitions, interpreting color, precise dragging, guessing whether a selection was registered, or losing the submitted response during feedback; the implementation avoids those demands.

## Evidence of learning

Evidence worth observing goes beyond a correct click:

- the pupil consults the role statement before or after manipulating the category token;
- the pupil can point to the phrase that supports the selected foreground concern;
- category and point of view remain coordinated when the valid category changes from public collective to private economic collective to private social individual;
- after an incoherent submission, the pupil changes the unsupported part and can say why the relationship is now stronger;
- the pupil does not treat a category as a guaranteed opinion and accepts that another actor could represent the same place differently;
- the third construction is made with less dependence on the scaffold while still using dossier evidence.

The prototype holds current selections and revisions in component memory only. It does not score, persist or transmit them.

## Core interaction hypothesis

> If the pupil repeatedly **places an actor in a category and orients a foreground lens** while the place representation visibly reorganises and feedback points back to **the actor’s authored role clue**, then the relationship between actor category, role and situated point of view becomes visible and usable without reducing a group to a fixed opinion.

## Exercise loop

1. Introduce actor, category and point of view, and show the two controls: place the actor token, then orient the lens.
2. Present a nearby place, one fictional actor, an explicit role statement and one visible connection to a farther nearby space.
3. Let the pupil select a composite category. The actor badge immediately changes to display all three classification axes.
4. Let the pupil select one foreground concern. The “table of views” immediately promotes that concern and labels the others as background, exposing the consequence before commitment.
5. Require the pupil to press **Relier ce regard**; selection alone never submits.
6. On an incoherent pair, preserve the submitted montage, explain whether the category, point of view or both conflict with current evidence, and offer a visible retry.
7. In retry, expose separate questions — “one person or an organisation?”, “who decides/owns?”, “what action word reveals the role?” — without selecting the answer.
8. After a coherent pair, explain the exact category and role-to-view link, then require a visible continuation action.
9. Repeat through all three authored variations and end in a distinct summary with no score or mastery claim.

## Deliberate progression and variations

This ordering is a prototype ramp, not an official year-by-year sequence and not a speed progression.

| Stable variation ID | Concept-relevant variation | Actor and role clue | Valid category | Valid foreground point of view | Planned marked incorrect pair |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| `place-commune` | Collective/public/political decision over a shared leisure and exchange space | **Conseil communal**: decides the square’s budget and shared-use rules | `collectif · public · politique` | “Budget et usages à partager” | `collectif · privé · économique` + “Livrer et vendre facilement” |
| `rue-epicerie` | Collective/private/economic supply role; explicit regional exchange | **Team of a neighbourhood grocery**: buys from farms, receives deliveries and sells locally | `collectif · privé · économique` | “Livraison et accès des clients” | `individuel · privé · social` + “Décider les règles pour tous” |
| `trajet-habitant` | Individual/private/social daily use; the actor is a person rather than an organisation | **Noé, resident**: crosses the square for the bus while accompanying his little sister | `individuel · privé · social` | “Un trajet continu et sûr” | `collectif · public · politique` + “Mettre en valeur les ventes” |

The invariant is not “public actors always think X”. It is: **classify from what the actor is; infer this point of view from what this actor does and says in this dossier**. The dimensions deliberately vary actor number, public/private status, role family, relationship to the place, thematic context (leisure/exchange, supply, habitat/mobility) and nearby/farther connection.

## Feedback and scaffolding

### Incorrect feedback

- Keeps both submitted choices visible in a “montage envoyé” strip.
- Names the selected category and/or foreground concern rather than saying only “faux”.
- Contrasts the unsupported choice with the exact current role clue.
- Uses the written cue **“Lien à reconstruire △”**, border pattern and text; color is supplementary.
- Offers one action, **Reprendre le montage**, without reload or automatic answer.

Examples are authored per dossier. For the grocery dossier, selecting the individual/social category yields: “La fiche parle d’une équipe qui achète et vend : l’acteur est ici une organisation privée à rôle économique.” Selecting public rule-making as its view yields: “Décider les règles pour tous appartient à un autre rôle ; ici, les verbes recevoir, acheter et vendre soutiennent la livraison et l’accès des clients.”

### Retry scaffold

- Separates category evidence into “une personne ou une organisation?”, “autorité publique ou acteur privé?” and “vie quotidienne, échanges économiques ou décisions collectives?”.
- Highlights role verbs, not the final option label.
- Asks which concern would change the actor’s ability to perform that role.
- Preserves the last submitted pair beside the new controls.
- Never auto-selects or auto-advances; the pupil must manipulate both controls and submit again.

### Correct feedback

- States the exact category and why it fits the actor’s status.
- States the exact point of view and quotes only the short authored clue from the dossier.
- Adds the guard: another actor could put another concern in the foreground.
- Uses the written cue **“Relation cohérente ✓”** in addition to color.
- Leaves feedback available until the pupil activates the continuation control.

Support is strongest on the first retry because category definitions are fully displayed. The same definitions remain concise and available in later variations; no timed fading is used.

## Complete content model and answer rules

### Authored data shape

Each of exactly three scenarios contains:

- a stable variation ID;
- a fictional nearby place and cross-space connection;
- one fictional actor and a bounded role statement;
- one correct composite category and one planned wrong category;
- exactly three authored point-of-view options, one correct and one planned wrong;
- category-specific and view-specific corrective explanations;
- one correct relationship explanation;
- three scaffold questions and highlighted role verbs.

There is no generator, randomness, remote content or unbounded fact model.

### Category vocabulary

Only these three composite profiles occur in this construction:

1. `individuel · privé · social` — one person acting through daily/shared life;
2. `collectif · privé · économique` — a non-government organisation acting through buying, selling or supply;
3. `collectif · public · politique` — a public authority acting through collective decisions and rules.

These composites are intentionally clear authored cases, not a claim that every real actor fits only one box.

### Valid-answer rule

A submission is correct **only if both**:

```text
selectedCategory === scenario.correctCategory
AND
selectedView === scenario.correctView
```

In the two-step builder, a category-only or view-only selection cannot enable submit. Any mismatch reaches incorrect feedback; partial correctness is named specifically. Selection changes the visible model but does not count as a submitted decision. A visible compact alternative presents two complete category–view relations for pupils who benefit from reading the link as one unit; activating either button explicitly deposits that complete authored relation and uses the same answer rule and feedback. It is not hidden, does not identify which proposal is correct, and does not bypass evaluation. A retry preserves the last submission and re-enables both ordinary response formats. Completion is reachable only after correct submissions for all three stable variation IDs and a continuation action after each.

### Edge cases and ambiguity guards

- All actors and places are fictional; no disputed local policy or personal data is implied.
- Role statements explicitly establish public/private status and action family, so correction does not depend on outside civic knowledge.
- Point-of-view options describe concerns in this dossier, not emotions, moral worth or immutable group beliefs.
- Feedback explicitly says another actor — or the same category in another situation — may foreground something else.
- No scenario asks the pupil to infer a real person’s private belief from identity alone.
- The “political” profile uses a council that actually decides budget/rules, avoiding an ambiguous technical employee case.
- The “economic” profile names buying, receiving and selling; the “social” profile names an individual daily route.
- The nearby/farther links are context, not facts needed to guess correctness.
- All answer labels remain visible at 320 px and are ordinary buttons; no exact placement, drag or hover is scored.
- The two complete relations in the compact alternative exchange A/B positions on the middle variation, preventing a fixed-position strategy.

## Interaction states and lifecycle

| State | Visible evidence and transition |
|---|---|
| `intro` | Purpose, definitions, two-step operation and unique start action. |
| `active` | Current variation, actor dossier, category manipulation, lens manipulation, visible consequence and marked submit. |
| `incorrect` | Preserved submitted pair, situation-specific written explanation, non-color cue and unique retry action. |
| `retry` | Preserved last submission, scaffold text, enabled category/lens controls and marked submit. |
| `correct` | Exact relationship explanation and preserved coherent pair. |
| `continuation` | Visible sibling region with the unique next/finish action; feedback remains readable. |
| `complete` | Distinct summary after three different variations reached correct feedback and were continued. |

The presentation-neutral `6H-SMOKE-1` path is `intro → active → incorrect → retry → correct + continuation`, then correct marked paths on later variations, then `complete`. Both marked attempts require two ordered controls (category step 1, point-of-view step 2) followed by the same marked submit. The checked-in legacy harness also follows the visible compact proposal buttons marked `data-smoke-answer`; those buttons are an ordinary coarse alternative, submit through `submitRelationship()`, and reach the same feedback, retry, continuation and completion states. Its legacy root/state attributes sit on a non-rendering `display: contents` compatibility ancestor outside `<main>`, so the 6H-SMOKE-1 prototype root contains only the exact state vocabulary and one visible region per current state/variation. No hidden control or test-only transition exists.

## Accessibility and devices

- All manipulation uses native `<button>` controls with `aria-pressed`; Tab reaches them in reading order and Enter/Space selects them.
- The category and lens groups have programmatic labels. Current choices are stated in visible text and in a polite live region.
- Submit is a separate native button and remains disabled until both required choices exist.
- Stage headings receive programmatic focus after start, feedback, retry, continuation and completion; a separate polite announcement reports the change.
- Focus has a high-contrast outline independent of selection color, and no focus trap exists.
- Selected cards include the word **Choisi** and a check symbol; correctness includes written/symbol cues, not color alone.
- Every pupil control is at least 48 px high with coarse spacing. There is no drag, hover-only information, drawing, sound or precision placement.
- The layout stacks into one reading column well before 320 CSS px. Long labels wrap, cards use `min-width: 0`, and the root clips decorative layers rather than content.
- At 320 px, all required controls fit the viewport width; vertical scrolling is allowed and the full recovery path remains available.
- Decorative lens motion is brief and nonessential. Under `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce`, transitions and transforms are removed while every state and consequence remains visible.
- Text is HTML, not embedded in an image. The decorative map is `aria-hidden`; all its essential meaning is repeated in text.

## Prototype boundaries

### Included

- One observable category–role–point-of-view relationship.
- Three fully authored local scenarios with visible links to nearby farther spaces.
- A two-part construction whose consequence changes before submission.
- Specific partial-error feedback, recoverable scaffold, continuation and completion.
- Keyboard, pointer and touch operation; actual 320 px and reduced-motion design.
- Component-memory state only, reset on reload or visible restart.

### Excluded

- Real municipalities, people, policy disputes or claims about actual actor opinions.
- A complete taxonomy of mixed or overlapping real-world actors.
- Negotiation between actors, ranking interests or choosing a “best” development.
- Independent free-text justification, map construction, source evaluation or teacher reporting.
- Scores, timers, rewards, identity, analytics, network calls, storage or result submission.
- Claims of mastery, measured learning, pupil validation or attainment of the end-of-cycle expectation.

## Observation plan

### What to observe

- Whether pupils read the actor/status evidence before selecting a composite category.
- Whether the foreground reordering prompts them to compare their own preference with the actor’s role.
- Which part they revise after partial feedback: category, view or both.
- Whether they can indicate the supporting verb or sentence after a correct construction.
- Whether they continue to use role evidence when the actor changes from organisation to person.
- Whether they interpret a composite category as a fixed opinion despite the explicit guard.
- Time and errors caused by locating controls, understanding `Choisi`, scrolling, or noticing the separate submit action.

### Evidence supporting the interaction hypothesis

The hypothesis gains support if pupils who first make an incoherent pair use the role clue to revise it, can explain the link in their own words, and coordinate the changed categories across the later authored cases without merely repeating one screen position. It is stronger if pupils spontaneously say that another actor could foreground a different concern in the same place.

### Evidence weakening or falsifying it

The hypothesis is weakened if pupils choose from visual position or profile keywords without consulting role evidence, if foreground movement adds no interpretable consequence, if they believe category alone guarantees an opinion, or if a coherent result disappears when answer positions/context change. It is also weakened if the composite categories create more confusion than they resolve.

### Distinguishing concept error from interface friction

- **Likely concept evidence:** the pupil can operate and describe both controls but links the point of view to a personal preference, misreads actor status despite rereading, or cannot explain why role evidence matters.
- **Likely interface friction:** the pupil does not notice which card is selected, expects selection to auto-submit, cannot find the submit button after scrolling, mistakes decorative layers for controls, or loses keyboard focus.
- Ask neutral probes: “Qu’est-ce qui te montre ton choix?”, “Quelle phrase du dossier as-tu utilisée?”, and “Qu’attendais-tu après ce bouton?” Do not label the pupil from the response.

### Remaining pupil-testing hypotheses

- Are three composite labels too dense for 6H even with plain-language titles?
- Does “privé” applied to an individual need more oral/contextual support?
- Does immediate foreground reordering clarify representation or look merely decorative?
- Does highlighting role verbs scaffold reasoning without giving away the answer?
- Can pupils distinguish their own view from the fictional actor’s view without feeling that one is judged morally superior?
- Is the mobile vertical distance between dossier, lens and submit manageable at 320 px?

## Findings and decisions

- **2026-07-15 — Initial construction decision:** use three fictional, explicitly evidenced actor roles rather than real local controversies. This bounds correctness and avoids inferring beliefs from identity.
- **2026-07-15 — Interaction decision:** require category and foreground lens as two manipulations, then a separate marked submission. Selection changes a coordinated representation immediately, but only submission produces correctness feedback.
- **2026-07-15 — Classification decision:** use three clear composite profiles in this study. Mixed and overlapping real actor identities remain outside the prototype rather than being silently forced into the answer model.
- **2026-07-15 — Evidence boundary:** all likely response patterns remain design hypotheses. No pupil observation, teacher review or validation has yet occurred; status remains `prototyping`.

## Promotion criteria

Promotion to classroom application work requires future evidence, not implementation completion alone:

- several 6H pupils can operate the complete keyboard/touch-equivalent loop and distinguish selection from submission without avoidable friction;
- observations show that role-linked manipulation can prompt revision and explanation, not only answer-position memorisation;
- pupils do not leave with the category-as-fixed-opinion interpretation, or the design is revised until that risk is acceptably controlled;
- pupils and an authorised teacher/curriculum reviewer find the category wording age-appropriate and the three answer models unambiguous;
- 320 px touch, keyboard, focus, non-color and reduced-motion paths pass independent review;
- catalog and full cluster smoke checks pass with the complementary practice and transfer/diagnosis studies;
- an independent 6H review records zero unresolved blocker/high findings and may then advance only to `testing`.

`validated` remains reserved for documented pupil trials plus authorised review outside this implementation slice.
